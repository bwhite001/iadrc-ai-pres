What is AI? The Simple Version

~3 min

Talking Points

  • Stress: it's free, and it works on any browser — no special computer

Script

So what actually is AI? I'm not going to explain the technology behind it — mostly because it's quite complicated and frankly not very interesting. What I am going to tell you is what it does, because that's what matters.

The simplest way I can describe it: imagine you had an Elmer — one of those incredibly knowledgeable, helpful people in the club who seems to know the answer to every radio question ever asked. It has read — and I mean actually processed and understood — millions of books, technical manuals, forum posts, and articles. Including, by the way, a great deal of material specifically about amateur radio.

And you access it the same way you'd send a text message. You open a web page, you type your question, and within about three seconds you get a detailed answer. No app to install. No special equipment. It works on the phone in your pocket right now.

Why Ham Radio + AI is a Perfect Match

~3 min

Talking Points

  • Four reasons: calculations, learning, writing, and 24/7 availability
  • The 3am DX opening example always gets a laugh and makes it real
  • Frame it as AI handling the boring background work so you have more time operating

Script

Ham radio is probably one of the best hobbies in the world for AI tools, and here's why.

First, our hobby involves a lot of technical calculations. Antenna lengths, matching networks, frequency maths — things that used to require a calculator, a handbook, and a bit of patience. AI handles all of that instantly, and it explains what it's doing as it goes.

Second, amateur radio is a hobby where you're always learning. There's always a new mode, a new band, a new piece of equipment to figure out. AI is the perfect study partner because it can explain anything at exactly the level you need — whether you're a brand new Foundation licensee or a thirty-year Advanced operator.

Third, the hobby involves a fair amount of writing — club newsletters, emails to DX stations, net scripts, meeting minutes. AI is outstanding at all of that. You give it the key points and it produces a well-written document in seconds.

And fourth — and this one always gets a nod — AI works at three o'clock in the morning. Pause. You know how it is. The DX station you've been chasing for months appears on the cluster, you work them, and then you wonder — did I have the right offset? Was my audio okay? What's the band plan for that country? No Elmer is awake at that hour. AI is always awake.

Alright, let's get into the practical stuff. Example one: antenna design.

Now, most of us know the formula — 468 divided by the frequency in megahertz — but we might not have it at our fingertips, and working it out and then figuring out what wire to buy and how to feed it takes time.

Here's what you'd type into ChatGPT: Read the prompt on screen aloud, slowly.

Notice a few things about that question. It mentions the location — Brisbane. It mentions the band — 40 metres. It mentions the physical constraints — eight metres high, two trees. The more specific you are, the better and more useful the answer.

Within seconds, AI will give you the wire lengths in both metres and feet, a materials list with Australian alternatives, suggestions for that specific height, and options for how to feed it — direct coax, with a balun, and so on. It takes three seconds and you don't need the ARRL Handbook open on the bench.

The 40m Half-Wave Dipole — Diagram

~2 min

Talking Points

  • Walk through the diagram: two arms, feed point, coax, insulators, supports
  • Each arm is about 10.2 metres — total 20.4 metres
  • The formula: 468 ÷ f(MHz) in feet — AI calculates this instantly
  • Mention the inverted-V option — only needs one centre support

Script

Here's what that 40-metre dipole actually looks like, for those who are more visual. You've got two arms going out from the centre feed point — each one roughly ten and a quarter metres, for a total of just over twenty metres. That's roughly the length of six car parks end to end — so it needs a bit of yard, but it's very achievable.

The centre feed point is where your coax connects. You'd run the coax down to the rig — and AI will tell you what length and what connector you need for your specific radio. At each end there's a small insulator, and then a rope or cord going up to whatever supports you're using — trees, poles, whatever you have available.

If you only have one tall support, you can run it as an inverted-V shape — centre high, ends lower — and it performs almost as well as a horizontal dipole. AI will explain all of this if you ask, and suggest the variation that suits your yard.

Example 2 — QSO Log Analysis

~2 min

Talking Points

  • Most logging software can export plain text or ADIF — copy and paste works
  • Even a few dozen QSOs is enough to get useful analysis
  • Works with ADIF, plain text, or even a photo of a paper log
  • AI finds patterns you'd never spot manually

Script

Example two: your QSO log. Most of us keep some kind of log — whether it's in Ham Radio Deluxe, Log4OM, or a good old notebook. If you export it to plain text — or even just copy a chunk of it — you can paste it straight into ChatGPT and start asking questions about it.

Ask things like: "What's my most active time of day?" Or "Which band do I work most contacts on?" Or "Show me all my DX contacts — which country have I worked the most?"

AI goes through the whole log and answers in seconds. It's the kind of analysis that would take you an hour with a spreadsheet, done instantly. And it helps you plan — if AI tells you that eighty percent of your Japan contacts happen between seven and nine in the morning on forty metres, that's useful information for your next operating session.

And yes — if you keep a paper log, you can take a photo of the page and upload it. AI can read the handwriting well enough to give you a useful analysis.

Example 4 — Schematic Troubleshooting

~2 min

Talking Points

  • Photo upload works in ChatGPT — you need a free account
  • AI narrows down the fault area — saves hours of frustration
  • Describe the symptoms clearly — the more detail, the better
  • Critical: always verify before touching mains-connected equipment

Script

Example four, and this one tends to get a lot of interest: fault-finding. We've all got an old rig in the shack that's developed a problem. Maybe it worked last year and now the TX power is down. Maybe the receiver is noisy on certain bands. Normally, you'd dig out the service manual, trace the circuit, and start prodding things with a multimeter — which can take days if you're not sure where to start.

Here's what you can do instead. Take a clear, well-lit photo of the schematic — just the relevant section — and upload it to ChatGPT. Then describe the fault: "RX works perfectly but TX output is very low on all bands. The driver stage is Q3. What should I check first?"

AI will usually suggest two or three specific places to investigate, explain what symptoms would confirm each fault, and tell you how to test them. It's not infallible — and we'll talk about that later — but it often points you in exactly the right direction and saves hours of frustration.

One important note: Emphasis here. Before you touch anything inside a mains-connected piece of equipment, please have it verified by someone with the right qualifications. AI can suggest where to look — it cannot replace a trained technician when mains voltages are involved.

Getting Better Answers — Prompt Tips

~2 min

Talking Points

  • The key skill is asking good questions — more detail = better answer
  • Four starter words cover 90% of ham radio uses: Explain, Compare, Rewrite, Summarise
  • Encourage them to take a photo of this slide with their phone
  • These four starters are on the handout if you've prepared one

Script

The single most useful skill you can develop with AI is knowing how to ask a good question. The good news is that AI is very forgiving — even a vague question usually gets a useful answer. But a specific, detailed question gets a much better one.

There are four words that will start almost every useful question you'll ever ask. Point to each card on screen as you say it.

"Explain." Use this when you want to understand something. "Explain SWR to me like I'm new to HF." "Compare." Use this when you're making a decision. "Compare the IC-7300 and IC-7610 for a beginner HF station." "Rewrite." Use this when you have some text that needs improving. "Rewrite this club notice to sound friendlier." "Summarise." Use this when you have a long document. "Summarise the key points of this antenna manual in dot points."

Those four words, combined with specific details about what you're working on, will get you ninety percent of what you need. If you have a printed handout: These four are on your handout — take it home and try one this week.

Propagation Prediction with WSPR

~3 min

Talking Points

  • WSPR = "whisper" — the pronunciation always surprises people
  • Low-power beacon, automated, worldwide reception reports
  • wspr.rocks shows a real-time world map of signal paths
  • AI helps interpret what the map means for your operating plans

Script

Let's look at something related to what AI just explained to you — WSPR, which is pronounced "whisper." Ask: How many of you have come across WSPR before? Wait for hands.

WSPR stands for Weak Signal Propagation Reporter. The idea is straightforward: a small transmitter — it can be as low as 200 milliwatts, less than a quarter of a watt — sends an automated beacon signal. Receiving stations all around the world listen for it, and when they hear it, they report it to a central website. That website then shows you, on a live world map, which stations heard your beacon and how strong the signal was.

The practical value for us is enormous. Before you call CQ on 40 metres looking for Japan, you can check WSPR and see whether any VK4 stations are currently being heard in JA. If they are — the band is open, get on and call. If not — either wait, or try a different band.

And here's where AI comes in again. You can take the WSPR data — copy a few rows from the website — paste it into ChatGPT and ask: "What does this tell me about band conditions from VK4 right now?" And it gives you a plain-English analysis.

Safety Tips for Using AI Online

~4 min

Talking Points

  • This is one of the most important slides — don't rush it
  • Scammers use AI brand names to set up fake websites
  • Real ChatGPT/Claude will never ask for banking details
  • Bookmark the two trusted sites — put them on screen for reference
  • Suggest a separate email address as a practical tip

Script

Now — and this is important, so I'd like everyone's attention for a moment. AI tools are genuinely useful, but as with anything on the internet, there are people out there trying to take advantage of others. So let's talk about how to stay safe.

First: only use the trusted sites. ChatGPT is at chat.openai.com. Claude is at claude.ai. Those are the two I'd recommend. Write them down or take a photo of the slide — both are on screen right now. That way you always come back to the right place.

Second: never enter sensitive personal information. That means no bank account details, no Medicare number, no passwords, no full home address. The legitimate AI tools — ChatGPT, Claude — don't need any of that information. They just need your question. If a website asks you for financial details to "unlock AI," close it immediately and ask a trusted person to have a look at it.

Third: be wary of fake AI websites. Scammers build websites that look like ChatGPT but aren't. They use similar names, similar logos, and they're designed to steal your login details. If you find yourself on an AI-looking site that you didn't bookmark yourself, treat it with caution.

Fourth: use a separate email if you can. It takes five minutes to create a free Gmail account specifically for AI tools. That way, if anything goes wrong, your main email — the one you use for banking and personal communication — is untouched.

What AI Gets Wrong

~4 min

Talking Points

  • "Hallucinations" — AI states wrong facts confidently — this is the key limitation
  • Training cutoff — newer regulations, equipment, band plans may be wrong
  • AI cannot hear audio, read meters, or see the shack in real time
  • The golden rule: verify important technical decisions with a human or authoritative source

Script

So we've seen a lot of what AI can do. Let's be equally honest about what it gets wrong. Because it does get things wrong, and it's important you know that before you rely on it.

The biggest issue is what people in the AI world call "hallucinations." This is a slightly odd word for it, but what it means is this: AI sometimes states something that is completely wrong — and it states it with total confidence, in the same calm, authoritative tone it uses when it's right. There's no flicker of uncertainty. It just says the wrong thing as if it's fact.

For ham radio, this could mean a wrong frequency allocation, an incorrect component value, a made-up part number, or an out-of-date band plan. So for anything technically important — anything you're going to act on — please verify it against a second source. The ARRL Handbook, the WIA website, your equipment manual, or a knowledgeable club member.

The second limitation is that AI's knowledge has a cutoff date. It was trained up to a certain point in time, and it doesn't automatically know about things that happened after that — new regulations, new equipment, new IARU band plans. Always check anything time-sensitive against a current source.

And third: AI cannot perceive the physical world. It can't hear the audio from your microphone, read your S-meter, or measure your SWR. It can only work with what you tell it. So when troubleshooting, describe as much as you can — and then verify what it suggests before you act on it.

The golden rule: use AI as a starting point, not as the final word.
